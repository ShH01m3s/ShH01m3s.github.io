Incident Response 🗒

domain: ir

doctype: article


Phases: Preparation, Detection & Analysis, Containment, Eradication & Recovery, Post Incident Activity. Event vs Incident. IDS alert may be considered as an event before it’s confirmed. Incident negatively affects the company. Unplanned interruption or reduction in quality of IT systems.

IR Team Models: Central (one for the entire company), distributed (per city, site etc), coordinating (assisting).

Removable media, attrition, web, email, impersonation, loss or theft of equipment.

Questions:

  • Who attacked you and why?
  • When and How did it happen?
  • Did this happen because of the poor sec policy and processes?
  • How widespread is the incident?
  • What steps are being taken to determine what happened and prevent it in future?
  • What’s the impact?
  • Was any personally identifiable information leaked?
  • What is the estimated cost of this incident?

NIST vs SANS IR frameworks: https://cybersecurity.att.com/blogs/security-essentials/incident-response-steps-comparison-guide

Common threats: software attacks, data exfiltration, sabotage and equipment theft.

Preparation

  1. Compile a list of all your assets (servers, networks, applications etc)
  2. Rank them by level of importance.
  3. Monitor their traffic patterns and create baselines.
  4. Create a communication plan (who to contact, how, and when based on each incident type).
  5. Determine which security events, and at what thresholds, these events should be investigated.
  6. Create an incident response plan for each type of incident.

Resources:

Incident Handler Communications and Facilities (contacts, on-call info, reporting mechanism, issue tracking, smartphones, encryption soft, war room, secure storage)

Incident Analysis Hardware and Software (DF workstations, backup devices, laptops, spare PCs, servers and net equipment, blank removable media, portable printer, packet sniffers and protocol analysers, DF software, removable media, evidence gathering accessories.

Incident Analysis Resources. Port lists, docs, net diagrams and list of assets, baselines, crypto hashes.

Best practices: risk assessment, host security, network security, malware prevention, user awareness and training.

Checklist:

  • Area all members aware of the sec policies?
  • Do all members of IR know whom to contact?
  • Do all of them have access to journals and toolkit?
  • Have they all participated in IR drills?

Useful to read: https://www.sans.org/reading-room/whitepapers/incident/documentation-incident-response-air-tank-scuba-diving-2021.

Creating a playbook

Detection and Analysis (SANS Identification)

The worst has happened. research mode. Gather everything you can on the the incident. Then analyse it. Determine the entry point and the breadth of the breach.

Precursors - signs that in incident may occur in the future. For example, logs show that a vulnerability scanner was used. Or a vulnerability was found in some software that’s used by the company.

Indicators - signs that an incident have occurred already or is occurring now. Malware detected by a anti-virus program. Configs changed, multiple failed logins etc.

IDS/IPS are used.

DLP - Data Loss Prevention. Set of tools and procedures that ensure that no data is lost, misused or accessed by unauthorised people.

SIEM - Security Information and Event Management solutions - SIM + SEM. Analysis of log data.

Documentation

Current status of the incident, summary, indicators, other incidents related, actions taken by all, chain of custody*, impact assessment, contacts, list of evidence, comments from incident handlers (IH), next steps to be taken (rebuild the host/upgrade).

Functional Impact Categories

None - nothing’s changed.

Low - the business can still provide its critical services but the efficiency is lost.

Medium - O. has lost an ability to provide its critical services to some users.

High - O. is no longer able to provide its critical services.

Information Impact Categories

None - no info was exfiltrated or compromised.

Privacy breach - sensitive PII was accessed or exfiltrated.

Proprietary breach - unclassified proprietary info exfiltrated (PCII).

Integrity loss - sensitive or proprietary info was changed or deleted.

Recoverability Effort Categories

TTR - time to recovery

Regular - TTR is predictable with existing resources.

Supplemented - TTR is predictable with additional resources.

Extended - TTR is unpredictable.

Not recoverable, launch the investigation.

Containment. Eradication. Recovery.

Containment aims to stop the bleeding. Here is where you patch the threat’s entry point.

Eradication aims to remove the threat.

Recovery aims to get the system operational if it went down or simply back to business as usual if it didn’t.

Evaluate potential damage and theft of resources, need for evidence preservation, service availability, time and resources to implement the strategy, its effectiveness, duration of solution.

For forensics get the image before anything, gather evidence and follow chain of custody.

Checklist:

  • Can the problem be isolated? Are all affected isolated? Have the forensic copied been created?
  • Can this system be reimaged and patched? Was all malware removed and the system hardened?
  • What tools are you going to use to test, monitor and verify that the systems being restored and are no longer compromised by the same cause?

For all evidence put down the following information:

  • Identifying information (location, serial, model, hostname, MAC and IP)
  • Name, title, phone of each individual who collected or handled the evidence
  • Time and date (+ time zone) of each occurrence of evidence handling
  • Location where the evidence were stored

Choosing a containment strategy:

  • Potential damage or theft of resources
  • Need for evidence preservation
  • service availability
  • time and resources to implement this strategy
  • effectiveness of the strategy (partial of full containment)
  • duration

learn from your experience so you can better respond to future security events.
