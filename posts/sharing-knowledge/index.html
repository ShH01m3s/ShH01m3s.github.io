Knowledge vs Kindness

April 7, 2026

  • ← Back to Neuroscience

What would most people choose: brains or kindness? It seems that this world promotes the latter, but our own evolutionary history might suggest there is more to kindness than meets the eye.

Nature rewards muscle and a cold intellect so that the fittest survive to pass winning genes on to their offspring. Unlike other animals collecting provisions, Homo sapiens thrived by collecting something more ephemeral—information. This asset, unlike nuts and berries, was impossible to preserve, let alone to pass on from generation to generation, until the invention of language. Standing on the shoulders of our ancestors, we were propelled by knowledge into the future with such breathtaking speed that, only a few thousand years after learning to write, we find ourselves pondering the dangers of artificial intelligence. At the same time, this very same miraculous asset may one day be our ultimate demise. Has knowledge really been our main evolutionary advantage?

Human’s history is the remarkable journey of a species that has wielded the power of knowledge to outmanoeuvre its physically more intimidating rivals. The importance of sharing that power among one’s own kin is hard to overestimate. Should a prehistoric Paul have kept to himself that he had seen his friend Gary eating a red berry and dying shortly after, half of their tribe would swiftly have followed in Gary’s footsteps the very next day. In the 1990s, Boyd and Richerson first emphasised the link between knowledge sharing and evolutionary success, and this argument is still hard to disprove. Imagine if you had to learn that snakes are often poisonous the hard way, or rediscover that two plus two equals four all by yourself? We would never have progressed beyond counting the wolves chasing us (if we were foolish enough to stop and do the counting). Knowledge sharing is our indisputable evolutionary advantage, which might have shaped us even more than we think. For example, some believe that knowledge sharing may have led to females living way past menopause, when most other species don’t endow theirs with such a lifespan.

The grandmother hypothesis offers a flattering explanation for why many women live past their reproductive years. For thousands of years, grandmothers have helped by gathering food, caring for younglings, and preserving knowledge. Not many species, though, can boast old grandmothers; most females die quite young. What if human grandmothers, with their unusual lifespan, helped pass down knowledge and assist in raising future generations? Not every woman today is happy to slave away for her cheeky grandchildren, but prehistoric women might have been crucial in human evolution. Whether a longer lifespan aided knowledge sharing to such a degree that it became an evolutionary trump card remains open to dispute.

Kachel, Premo, and Hublin (2011) argued that, historically, people died young at around forty; therefore, few women lived long enough for “grandmothering” to even matter. That means that menopause could simply be a by-product of living long, in part due to improving medical knowledge. Herndon and Walker (2010) of Emory University find these conclusions feeble and point out that humans and primates age differently and suffer from different deseases insisting that the selection for an extended lifespan was not random.

Even though we cannot challenge the role of knowledge, we can, however, challenge its omnipotence. Rival human species, as well as some mammals, also used tools and passed on knowledge to their offspring. Neanderthals were not as dim-witted as the media likes to depict them and, surprisingly, had larger brains. If strength and intellect are the main driving forces, why, then, did humans outcompete those who were stronger and smarter? There might be a more compelling explanation. A novel idea of human domestication was put forward by researchers from Duke University (Hare et al., 2012). It turns out that domestication—“friendliness,” in short—can be traced in the changes to our anatomy. Friendliness is literally imprinted on our faces! Researchers noticed that those first “friendly” touches in the faces of our distant ancestors are missing in the superior Neanderthals, which are now extinct. Hare and his team speculate that our friendliness between groups led to wider knowledge sharing, putting us on the evolutionary pedestal.

In an attempt to pin down the role of friendliness, Hare and his team compared chimpanzees with bonobos—their friendlier relatives. Chimpanzees, living in highly competitive and dangerous environments, often kill members of their own community, even infants, in violent outbursts. Peace-loving bonobos, on the other hand, spoiled by the cornucopia of the Congo River, don’t need to compete with other species, and so aggression is not as central to their survival. As a result, they found friendliness and cooperation much more rewarding. In their book Survival of the Friendliest, Brian Hare and Vanessa Woods thrown in an idea that human success is rooted in this same tendency to choose cooperation over aggression, but going even further than bonobos by extending that cooperation beyond one’s own tribe. Unlike scowling chimpanzees that haven’t moved much up the evolutionary ladder, humans might have found a new way of adapting—potentially an evolutionary game-changer.

However much we may want to believe that our wits made us the fittest, that cannot be the whole story. Skillful as we are in solving scientific puzzles, our own history remains a mystery. Many have tried to solve it, and many have failed. Perhaps knowledge and strength were indeed our most reliable weapons against fanged beasts lurking in the eerie shadows of prehistoric forests. However, it’s been a while since furry or slithering neighbours were humans’ biggest concern (unless you live in Australia). That niche has long been occupied by other humans. Today, holding our breath, we watch the petty squabbling of powerful men flaunting their nuclear weapons. Perhaps there is a simple, grounded wisdom in the golden rule shared by many religions: be kind to your neighbour. Perhaps kindness is the new kind of evolution, and failing to appreciate it may one day cost humanity its existence.
